Great Myths of the Great Depression

A visual summary of Lawrence W. Reed's long argument that the Great Depression was not a self-destruction of capitalism, but a prolonged crisis built and extended by policy mistakes.

01 · Whole Judgment

The essay reverses the standard classroom story.

Reed rejects the claim that market capitalism caused the crash and that government rescue ended it.

He treats 1929 to 1941 as four stacked policy failures: monetary contraction, tariff-led trade collapse, New Deal controls, and Wagner Act labor coercion.

In this reading, durable recovery arrives only when policy pressure eases and private investment returns.

4 Policy phases

The essay breaks the Depression into four linked downturns rather than one single crash.

12 Years of slump

The central claim is that intervention turns a normal recession into a crisis lasting roughly 1929 to 1941.

25% Peak joblessness

The text points to unemployment above one quarter of the labor force in 1933.

30% Money shrink

It highlights a one-third contraction of the money supply after 1929 as the first major blow.

02 · Phase Structure

Four policy turns that, in the essay's telling, make a crash last a decade.

Phase Policy move Damage path Author's result
Phase I Monetary policy and the business cycle 1920s to 1933 The Fed feeds an easy-credit boom in the 1920s and then tightens sharply after 1928. The money supply falls by 30 percent, real rates rise, and the correction becomes a deep contraction. The stock crash appears as a symptom of policy distortion rather than the originating cause.
Phase II Disintegration of the world economy 1930 to 1933 Hoover backs Smoot-Hawley, leans on firms to keep wages high, expands spending, and signs the 1932 tax increase. Tariffs trigger retaliation, exports fall, farms and rural banks fail, and firms cannot absorb labor at falling prices. Unemployment rises from 8.9 percent in 1930 to more than 25 percent in 1933.
Phase III The New Deal 1933 to 1937 Roosevelt closes banks, seizes gold, devalues the dollar, destroys crops under AAA, and cartelizes industry under NRA. Prices, output, and wages are pushed away from market adjustment while spending and taxation move higher. The essay treats the New Deal as an anti-revival program that interrupts an early rebound.
Phase IV The Wagner Act 1935 to 1941 The Wagner Act strengthens unions while new taxes, Fed reserve hikes, and court-packing fears darken the business climate. Strikes, legal privilege, and policy uncertainty keep capital on the sidelines and raise the cost of hiring. The 1937 to 1938 relapse becomes a depression within a depression.
03 · Mechanism

How a recession becomes a 12-year slump in the essay's logic.

01

Cheap credit

Artificially low rates support projects that look sound only inside an easy-money boom.

02

Hard reversal

Sharp tightening removes liquidity before those fragile investments can unwind gradually.

03

Trade fracture

Tariffs and tax hikes cut export demand, shrink incomes, and spread bank failures through agriculture and small towns.

04

Administered prices

NRA codes, AAA controls, and wage pressure block the normal adjustment of prices, output, and labor costs.

05

Capital retreats

Union privilege, new taxation, and legal uncertainty make private investment retreat.

06

Recovery resets

With investment weak, each partial rebound loses force and the slump starts over.

04 · Exit Claim

Where the essay places recovery, and what lesson it wants the reader to keep.

1935-1936

When the Supreme Court voids the NRA and AAA, the economy shows some signs of life.

1937-1938

The Wagner Act, new taxes, reserve requirement hikes, and court-packing fears push the rebound back down.

Postwar

Sustained prosperity arrives only when investors face a less hostile climate and private capital moves back into production.

The essay's final political moral

  • Government mistakes can start a downturn and also keep it from clearing.
  • Programs sold as stabilization can deepen uncertainty and delay real hiring.
  • The closing aim is openly polemical: defend free enterprise against the standard myth of rescue by intervention.

Postscript warning

The postscript extends the same frame to 2008, warning that bailouts, monetary expansion, and higher tariffs repeat the political reflexes the essay blames for the 1930s.
