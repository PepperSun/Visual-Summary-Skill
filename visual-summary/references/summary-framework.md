# Summary Framework

Use this framework to rebuild a source document's structure.

## Classification axes

| Axis | Question | Values | Required | Main + secondary allowed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Form | What language mechanism drives the text? | narrative / argumentation / lyric / record | yes | yes |
| Intent | What effect does the author seek? | inform / explain / persuade / resonate / entertain / preserve | yes | yes |
| Structure | How is content organized? | chronology / space / logic / taxonomy / comparison / nested / free | yes | yes |
| Type | Subtype inside the form | depends on form | optional | no |

## Form diagnosis

| Form | Feature | Diagnostic question | Confusion point |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrative | advances through events, time, people | If the timeline is removed, is little left? | distinguish from examples inside argumentation |
| Argumentation | advances through claims, evidence, reasoning | Is the trunk one or more debatable propositions? | mixed works should be judged by the trunk |
| Lyric | advances through feeling, imagery, impression | If emotional color is removed, is little left? | narrative centers events; lyric centers feeling |
| Record | preserves facts or states with little interpretation | Is the author interpreting? | inform is for transmission; record is for future retrieval |

A first-person essay organized around a debatable claim belongs to argumentation even when it opens with scenes or anecdotes.

## Intent and skeletons

| Intent | What the text does | Diagnostic question | Skeleton |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inform | lets the reader know facts | Does it still work if the why is removed? | who / what / when / where / numbers |
| Explain | lets the reader understand mechanism | Is the trunk causal or mechanical? | phenomenon / mechanism / causal chain / boundary conditions |
| Persuade | makes the reader believe or act | Is there an opposing view to defeat? | use persuasion subtype skeletons |
| Resonate | makes the reader feel | Is the main effect emotional? | core feeling / imagery / insight |
| Entertain | creates immersion or pleasure | Is the main value readability or plot? | setting / turn / ending |
| Preserve | stores information for later lookup | Is it for future self or others? | decision / action item / owner / time |

## Explanation subtypes

| Subtype | Answers | What the text does | Diagnosis | Skeleton |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phenomenon explanation | why X happened | finds causes | explains a result | phenomenon / causal chain / boundary / counterexample |
| Mechanism explanation | how X works | describes system operation | decomposes a system | input / process / output / failure mode |
| Concept explanation | what X is | defines a concept | clarifies meaning | definition / attributes / boundary / example |
| Historical or evolutionary explanation | how X became current | explains path | emphasizes time change | start / key nodes / evolution path |

## Persuasion subtypes

For persuasive texts, the output must include the author's preference ranking or core positive claim.

| Subtype | Main action | Diagnosis | Skeleton |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stance persuasion | presents a claim and reasons | no specific opponent | claim / reason chain / evidence / implied meaning |
| Rebuttal persuasion | dismantles an opponent | main body replies point by point | core dispute / rebuttal table / rebuttal methods / reaffirmed core claim or residual judgment |
| Synthesis persuasion | combines positive claim and opponent rebuttal | both positive claim and specific opponent exist | positive claim + mechanism / rebuttal table / key evidence / implied meaning |

Required rebuttal table columns: opponent claim / author rebuttal / key evidence / residual judgment. Opponent claims must be concrete source-level propositions, not metaphors, labels, or generic abstractions.

For rebuttal persuasion that also restates the author's own thesis, the first section should identify the critique being answered. Put the reaffirmed positive thesis at the end, after the rebuttal table and rebuttal method, so the reader sees what the author preserves through the rebuttal.

Common rebuttal methods:

| Method | Function |
|---|---|
| Identify misreading | show that the criticism was already addressed |
| Distinguish concepts | split two ideas the opponent merged |
| Give counterexample | disprove with a concrete case |
| Ask for mechanism | require a causal chain the opponent has not supplied |

## Structure diagnosis

| Structure | Feature | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Chronology | time sequence, including reversal and key nodes | evolution belongs here |
| Space | organized by place or position | uncommon as sole structure |
| Logic | cause-effect, whole-part, problem-solution | mechanism is content; logic is organization |
| Taxonomy | categories, lists, parallel items | comparison requires deeper contrast |
| Comparison | A vs B or several deep contrasts | multiple contrast groups still count as comparison |
| Nested | story inside story or layered structure | identify levels clearly |
| Free | stream of consciousness or no clear skeleton | avoid unless hidden structure cannot be found |

Use a logic chain when the trunk advances through causality or inference. When points, categories, cases, or dimensions unfold in parallel rather than through causality, inference, or time, prefer a table.

## Type options

| Form | Type options |
|---|---|
| Narrative | detective / science fiction / satire / romance / historical / realism / fable / literary nonfiction / biography |
| Argumentation | review / empirical / methodology / commentary / stance / case study / theory building |
| Lyric | lyrical essay / philosophical essay / travel writing / memoir |

## Forced table rules

| Parallel structure | Required columns |
|---|---|
| Opponent claim vs author rebuttal | opponent claim / author rebuttal / evidence / residual judgment |
| Option A vs option B | dimension / A / B |
| Old method vs new method | dimension / old / new |
| N categories in parallel | category / definition / feature / case |
| Multiple cases compared | case / controlled variable / unique difference / result |

## Non-narrative output format

Use 3 to 5 sections according to the source. For short sources, use exactly 3 sections. Treat Chinese sources under about 2,000 characters or English sources under about 1,500 words as short sources.

Compression rule: section 1 may use 1 to 3 short sentences. Sections 2 and later must keep each bullet, row, evidence point, and table cell to one simple sentence.

### 1. Core conclusion

Use 1 to 3 sentences. For stance persuasion, include the author's preference ranking. For rebuttal persuasion, state the dispute or critique being answered; if the author restates a positive thesis through the rebuttal, reserve that thesis for the final section. For explanatory writing, a neutral conclusion is acceptable.

### 2. Auxiliary structure

Use the structure type to select the format:
- chronology: stage table
- logic: mechanism table, reasoning table, or logic chain
- taxonomy: category table
- comparison: comparison table covering execution, cost, organization, and decision-making when relevant

Prefer chain evidence, such as evolution, trend, or cumulative change, over isolated facts. Use a logic chain for causality or inference. When points, categories, cases, or dimensions unfold in parallel rather than through causality, inference, or time, prefer a table. Keep auxiliary structures controlled, normally no more than 6 rows or bullets unless the source itself requires more. Each row, bullet, or cell should contain only one direct sentence.

### 3. Core mechanism

Explain the mechanism, evolution logic, classification logic, or comparison logic that supports section 2. Keep each point to one direct sentence.

### 4. Key clarification

Use this only when the article emphasizes a key node, process, boundary condition, rebuttal, or rejected interpretation that has not already been covered in section 2 or 3. If using a logic chain, keep it to no more than 8 nodes. Keep each clarification to one direct sentence. Omit this section when it would overlap with prior sections.

### 5. Supplement

Use only for important secondary claims, caveats, or context that do not repeat earlier content. Do not use this section as a standalone evidence list; evidence belongs inside the section whose claim it supports. Use one direct sentence per item. Omit the supplement when no distinct material remains.

For rebuttal texts that reaffirm the author's own thesis, use the final section to synthesize the author's core positive claim. This final section should not add new evidence or repeat the rebuttal table; it should state what the author has defended after dismantling the opponent.

### Section title rule

Rendered section titles must be specific to the source and follow the source's main movement. For rebuttal persuasion, titles should name the dispute, rebuttal target, or rebuttal move rather than generic framework labels. Do not use generic labels such as “Core mechanism”, “Key clarification”, or “Supplement” in a rendered page unless they are also meaningful for that specific source. When in doubt, calibrate section titles against `references/cases-summary-reference.md`, but do not copy them mechanically.

## Evidence and overlap rules

Evidence belongs inside the section whose claim it supports. Do not create a standalone evidence catalogue unless the source is itself organized that way or the user explicitly asks for one.

Do not repeat the same boundary condition, rebuttal, caveat, or residual judgment across multiple sections. If a boundary or rebuttal is already handled in a table, omit the separate clarification section unless the source gives it independent weight.

When a source rejects or limits an interpretation, include that rejected interpretation only once, either in the main table, a boundary callout, or the final residual judgment.

## Language and voice rules

Follow the source language unless the user requests English, Simplified Chinese, or Traditional Chinese. Use compact third-person objective voice in the final summary, including for first-person sources.

Use compact wording. Avoid generic AI phrases, overexplaining, and stiff analyst prose. In English, prefer simple syntax and restrained wording. In Chinese, prefer concise phrasing and avoid formulaic sentence frames such as “不是……而是……”, “如果说……则是……”, stacked negative openings, overextended metaphor chains, and prose-level colons or em dashes.

Negative Chinese patterns to avoid:
- “身份设定不是在告诉模型‘怎么写’，而是在告诉它‘你是谁’。”
- “这不是简单的‘编’，而是一种更高级、更具欺骗性的幻觉。”
- “不是因为它‘知道更多’，而是因为它会在回答前先‘想一想’。”
- “如果说案例 1 验证了……案例 2 要验证的则是另一个更微妙的变量。”
- “它做的事情更像是一个遥控器……那是天线的事，不是遥控器能管的。”

When using a metaphor, keep it brief and stop once the comparison has clarified the point. Do not extend one metaphor into a multi-link analogy chain. In body prose, prefer periods or commas over colons and em dashes unless a label, table, or literal source formatting requires them.

For lyric, memoir, diary-like, or strongly first-person essays, summarize the movement of feeling, image, and perception in third-person objective voice without flattening the text into abstract external interpretation.

## Speech and commentary handling

For argumentation with a clear top-level judgment and direct, oral, or commentary-like delivery:
- make section 1 the whole-content judgment when the source has a clear top-level claim
- write section 1 in 2 or 3 short sentences or lines
- keep the header metadata-only; do not place the main summary directly under the title
- move history or chronology later when it supports the judgment rather than forming the trunk
- decide whether a comparison is the trunk or only support; if support, state the final judgment first
- place dashboard, big-numeral, or at-a-glance panels after the main conclusion unless the source is primarily quantitative

## Content priority hierarchy

Apply this to every document type. The three levels are not equal — they have different retention rules.

### Level 1: Core claim
The judgment, thesis, or position the author returns to repeatedly. Identifiable by repetition across sections, explicit restatement, and placement at the opening or close of the argument.

Retention rule: always preserve in full. Do not compress, paraphrase loosely, or bury in a supporting role. If the source has a single dominant claim, it should be the most prominent element in the output.

### Level 2: Logic chain
The sequence of reasoning steps, sub-claims, or causal links the author uses to arrive at the core claim. Each step in the chain is a proposition, not an example.

Retention rule: always preserve the chain as a whole. The structure of reasoning is as important as the conclusion it produces. Represent each step clearly. A summary that states the conclusion but omits the logic chain has lost the most useful part of the document.

Diagnostic: a logic chain step answers "why" or "how" the core claim holds. An example or case answers "for instance." Keep the former; filter the latter.

### Level 3: Supporting evidence
Cases, anecdotes, statistics, quotations, and data points used to back individual steps in the logic chain. These are the most expendable layer.

Retention rule: for each logic chain step, select at most 1 strongest piece of evidence by default and drop the rest. Use 2 only when the two pieces do different work. Prefer quantified data over narrative cases when both are available. Prefer the case that is most concrete, most extreme, or most counterintuitive — whichever gives the step the most traction with a skeptical reader.

Do not let evidence accumulation substitute for logical clarity. A step supported by one strong example reads better than the same step buried under four cases.

### Summary feel target
The output should feel complete at the claim and logic level, and alive at the evidence level. It should not feel exhaustive at any level. A reader who finishes the summary should understand what the author concluded and why, with one vivid anchor or at most two — not a catalogue of the author's research.

## Narrative output format

Use three layers.

### 1. Story overview

Use 2 to 3 sentences: who the protagonist is, what happened, and what state they end in.

### 2. Story structure line

Use this table. Do not turn it into social interpretation or causal explanation.

| Structure point | Content |
|---|---|
| Protagonist | who |
| Initial state | starting point |
| Trigger event | what breaks balance |
| Progression | development |
| Turning point | key change |
| Ending state | final state |

### 3. Plot driver

| Driver type | Use case |
|---|---|
| Anomaly | suspense or absurdity |
| Conflict | drama or opposition |
| Choice | character decision |
| Relationship change | romance or interpersonal story |
| Information revelation | nonfiction or investigation |
| Cognitive change | psychological or inner story |
